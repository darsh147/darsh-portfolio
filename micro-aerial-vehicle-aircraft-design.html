Internship - Feynman June 2023

From Theory to CAD: Conceptualizing a High-Efficiency Micro Aerial Vehicle (MAV)

Feynman Aerospace

Iterative Aircraft Design

During my recent internship at Feynman Aerospace, I was tasked with a challenge that sits at the heart of aerospace engineering: balancing aerodynamic performance with structural practicality. My objective was to conceptualize a micro-class, fixed-wing RC aerial vehicle, taking it from initial physics-based constraints to a fully realized 3D CAD model.

Here is a deep dive into the engineering journey of designing a 1.5-meter wingspan MAV.

1. The Design Blueprint: Constraints & Objectives

Every successful design begins with a clear mission profile. For this project, the constraints were specific:

Wingspan 150 cm
Target Weight 1.0 - 1.5 kg
Operational Speed 10 - 15 m/s
Primary Goal Maximize payload & L/D efficiency

2. The Physics of Lift: Selection of the Main Airfoil

The first critical decision was choosing the right airfoil. An airfoil’s geometry determines how pressure is distributed across the wing, which directly dictates lift and drag performance.

The Mathematical Requirement

To stay airborne, the lift generated (L) must equal the aircraft's weight (W). I used the fundamental lift equation to determine my target Coefficient of Lift (CL):

L = ½ ρ V2 S CL
ρ (Density) 1.293 kg/m³
V (Velocity) 10 m/s
S (Area) 1.5 m²
L (Lift) 1.5 kg

Solving for CL, I determined that a minimum coefficient of 0.015 was required for level flight.

Iterative Analysis: NACA 2414 vs. NACA 1412

Initially, I considered the NACA 2414 airfoil. However, XFLR5 analysis revealed a CL of approx. 0.2 at a 0° angle of attack, which provided excessive lift for this specific lightweight requirement.

I pivoted to the NACA 1412. This thinner profile offered two strategic advantages:

  • Aerodynamic Alignment: A CL just below 0.2 at 0°, which was more compatible with the aircraft's performance requirements.
  • Weight Optimization: The reduced thickness profile decreases wing weight and material costs, directly increasing the available payload capacity.

3. Stability and Control: The Tail Configuration

For the horizontal and vertical stabilizers, I selected the NACA 0009 airfoil.

  • 1
    Why Symmetrical? Being a symmetrical airfoil, it generates zero lift at a 0° angle of attack, ensuring the tail doesn't introduce unwanted pitch moments during steady flight.
  • 2
    Drag Minimization: Its thin profile is ideal for reducing parasitic drag on the stabilizing surfaces.

To ensure maneuverability, I analyzed the control surfaces with a 20-degree deflection for the rudder and elevator to simulate high-authority control during takeoff and landing phases.

4. Advanced Wing Analysis: XFLR5 Insights

Moving beyond 2D airfoils, I conducted a Complete Wing Analysis to observe how the main wing and tail interacted. Using XFLR5, I modeled the wing with its designated mass and a 1.5m wingspan.

Key metrics monitored included:

  • CL/CD Ratio: This is the ultimate "efficiency" metric. A higher ratio indicates the aircraft can glide further or stay aloft longer with less power.
  • Induced Drag: Analyzed through streamlines to minimize energy loss from wingtip vortices.

5. From Analysis to Reality: CAD Design in SolidWorks

The final phase was translating these aerodynamic findings into a manufacturable model using SolidWorks.

  • Fuselage Integration: I designed the fuselage to accommodate the full avionic system and internal payload, ensuring the Center of Gravity (CG) remained within the stable range calculated during analysis.
  • Structural Detail: The wings were modeled with internal rib structures to ensure a balance between structural integrity and the 1.5 kg weight limit.

Conclusion

This internship at Feynman Aerospace was a masterclass in the Iterative Design Process. By combining theoretical fluid dynamics with modern computational tools like XFLR5 and SolidWorks, I was able to optimize a conceptual aircraft for both efficiency and stability.

While the aircraft remains a virtual prototype, the data-driven methodology used to create it provides a robust foundation for future physical fabrication and flight testing.
