Engineering the Next Generation of Spaceflight: A Modular 100 kN LCH4/LOX Propulsion System

Propulsion System Design

Modular Rocket Engine Design

In the modern "New Space" era, the demand for flexible and cost-effective launch systems is increasing rapidly. Traditional propulsion systems are often designed for a single vehicle configuration, which limits their adaptability to different mission profiles and results in long design cycles and high development costs.

My project, "Design and Analysis of 100 kN Rocket Propulsion System for Multi-Variant Launch Vehicles," conducted at the Indian Institute of Technology Bombay, tackles these challenges through a modular architecture. This system is designed to support multiple launch vehicle configurations, aiming to reduce research and development (R&D) time by approximately 20% and production costs by around 10%.

1. Thermochemical Optimization: The Methane Advantage

The system employs a high-performance cryogenic propellant combination: Liquid Methane (LCH4) and Liquid Oxygen (LOX). Methane is increasingly favored for next-gen launch systems due to its high density, clean-burning properties (low coking), and potential for reusability.

Chemical Equilibrium and Performance

Using NASA CEA (Chemical Equilibrium with Applications), I modeled the combustion characteristics. The propellants were analyzed at specific cryogenic temperatures: LCH4 at 111.64 K and LOX at 90.17 K.

A key engineering decision was the operating Oxidizer-to-Fuel (O/F) ratio, which was set at 2.600. This corresponds to an oxidizer excess coefficient (αox) of 0.652, indicating fuel-rich combustion relative to the stoichiometric O/F of 3.989. Fuel-rich combustion is critical for reducing the average molecular weight of exhaust gases and managing combustion temperatures to protect engine hardware.

Defining Combustion Efficiency (C*)

The Characteristic Velocity (C*) is a fundamental metric of combustion efficiency, representing the energy release per unit mass of propellant independent of the nozzle geometry. It is defined as:

C* = (Pc · At) / ṁ
Pc Chamber pressure.
At Throat area.
ṁ Propellant mass flow rate.
  • Theoretical C*: 1842.49 m/s.
  • Delivered C* (accounting for losses): 1778.57 m/s.

2. High-Velocity Gas Dynamics: Nozzle Optimization

The nozzle converts high-pressure combustion energy into directed kinetic thrust. I designed the contour geometry using Rocket Propulsion Analysis (RPA) with an expansion ratio (ε) of 35:1, optimized for sea-level ambient conditions.

Supersonic Validation via CFD

To ensure performance across varying atmospheric conditions, I conducted CFD simulations in ANSYS Fluent. The simulations validated the smooth acceleration of flow from subsonic to supersonic speeds:

  • Throat Mach Number: Exactly 1.0 (Choked flow).
  • Exit Mach Number: 2.1436.
  • Exit Velocity: Matched the theoretical prediction of 2340.27 m/s.
  • Specific Impulse (Isp): This "fuel economy" metric reached a delivered vacuum Isp of 266.73 s and a sea-level Isp of 223.14 s.

The lack of significant shock-induced flow separation confirms stable performance across different launch vehicle variants.

3. Structural Mechanics: Pressure Vessel Physics

The propulsion system requires robust cryogenic tanks capable of holding propellants under intense operating pressures. I designed these in SolidWorks and performed Finite Element Analysis (FEA) in ANSYS Mechanical.

Thin-Walled Pressure Vessel Equations

For the propellant tanks, I calculated the stresses using thin-walled pressure vessel theory. Under an internal operating pressure (P) of 12 atm, the walls experience two primary stresses:

Hoop Stress (σθ)

The stress acting tangentially to the circumference.

σθ = (P · r) / t

Longitudinal Stress (σL)

The stress acting parallel to the tank axis.

σL = (P · r) / (2t)

Where r is the tank radius and t is the wall thickness.

FEA and Material Safety

I selected a high-performance Aluminum-Lithium (Al-Li) alloy, prized in aerospace for its strength-to-weight ratio and cryogenic compatibility.

  • Max Von Mises Stress: 145 MPa
  • Material Yield Strength: ~480 MPa
  • Factor of Safety (FoS): 3.3
  • Max Deformation: 0.2 mm (ensuring stable operation)

4. Systems Engineering: The Power of Modularity

The core innovation lies in the Modular Structural Frame. Rather than a fixed design, I modeled an integrated stage that allows for the rapid interchange of engines and tanks.

  • Mass Distribution: I conducted a center of gravity (CoG) analysis to ensure the vehicle remains stable across different propellant capacities and mission profiles.
  • Integration Efficiency: This modular approach introduced ease-of-assembly features that reduce integration time and complexity.
  • Operational Impact: CAD-based estimates indicate a 15–20% reduction in assembly time compared to traditional fixed-frame designs.

Conclusion: Data-Driven Performance

This project demonstrates that high-performance rocketry and economic efficiency can coexist. By combining NASA CEA for thermochemical optimization, ANSYS Fluent for fluid dynamics, and ANSYS Mechanical for structural validation, I have engineered a 100 kN propulsion system that is adaptable, robust, and optimized for the next generation of launch vehicles.
