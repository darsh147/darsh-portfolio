Engineering the Future of Flight: A Deep Dive into Active Thrust Vector Control (TVC)

Project GARUD

Stabilization via TVC

In the landscape of modern aerospace engineering, the ability to manipulate vehicle attitude without relying on aerodynamic surfaces is a definitive milestone. While conventional model rockets depend on fixed-nozzle propulsion and aerodynamic fins for stabilization, Thrust Vector Control (TVC) offers a dynamic approach to governing a rocket's orientation by redirecting engine thrust. This project, titled "GARUD," represents a comprehensive journey through the design, simulation, and testing of a TVC-enabled model rocket, inspired by the groundbreaking missions of SpaceX’s Falcon series.

1. Theoretical Framework: Stability and Control Dynamics

At the heart of any rocket's flight is the interplay between the Center of Pressure (CP) and the Center of Gravity (CG). For a rocket to remain stable, the CP must typically lie beneath the CG, creating a restoring force that corrects deviations. However, TVC-controlled rockets often operate in regimes where aerodynamic fins are absent or ineffective.

In our design, rotation occurs around the Center of Gravity, while the CP acts as the focal point where aerodynamic forces converge. By gimbaling the motor, we induce a controlled torque that shifts the thrust vector, effectively creating a "virtual stability" that allows the rocket to maintain a vertical trajectory regardless of wind gusts or atmospheric disturbances.

2. Mathematical Modeling: The PID Control Algorithm

To bridge the gap between mechanical hardware and stable flight, we implemented a Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller. This algorithm serves as the core of the flight computer's architecture, chosen for its adaptability and efficiency.

The control law is mathematically defined as:

U(t) = Kpe(t) + Ki∫e(t)Δt + Kd(de/dt)
Proportional (Kp) Adjusts the actuator output based on the current tilt error.
Integral (Ki) Accumulates the error over time to eliminate steady-state offsets.
Derivative (Kd) Responds to the rate of change of the error, providing a damping effect that prevents overshooting.

To ensure the PID was grounded in physical reality, we meticulously calculated the Moment of Inertia using a dual-string vertical suspension mechanism. By measuring the time for multiple oscillations, we derived the fundamental parameters necessary for high-fidelity MATLAB Simulink simulations.

3. Structural Design and Additive Manufacturing

The mechanical integrity of GARUD was achieved through a fusion of traditional materials and advanced 3D printing techniques:

  • Rocket Body: Utilized a cardboard body tube for optimal strength-to-weight ratio, paired with a 3D-printed PLA nose cone.
  • Gimbal System: The TVC mount features an inner and outer gimbal design that allows for precise ±5° thrust vectoring in the x and z directions.
  • Print Optimization: Components were printed using PLA at a temperature range of 190-210°C. For critical structural parts like the TVC mount, a 50% infill and a 0.2mm layer width were utilized to ensure the assembly could withstand the forces of the E6-3 motor.

4. Avionics: The Digital Central Nervous System

The flight computer was built from the ground up using the Arduino Nano as the central processing unit. The avionics suite included:

  • Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU): An MPU6050 sensor provided 3-axis gyroscope and accelerometer data to estimate the rocket's attitude.
  • Altimeter: A BMP280 barometric pressure sensor with ±1 meter accuracy was used to track altitude and trigger landing leg deployment.
  • Data Logging: An SD Card Module captured real-time flight metrics for post-mission analysis.
  • Actuation: Two SG90 servo motors executed the PID's commands, tilting the motor mount with a speed of 60 degrees in 0.12s.

5. Launch Analysis and "Hard-Won" Engineering Lessons

The final flight test was conducted on May 30, 2024, at 11:13 am IST. While simulations predicted a peak altitude of 11 meters, the rocket reached approximately 0.5 meters.

Root Cause Analysis:

A post-flight investigation revealed that an accidental ignition occurred, causing both the ascent and descent rocket motors to fire simultaneously. This resulted in an immediate peak in thrust that overwhelmed the stabilization computer and caused thermal damage to the internal electronics.

While the flight did not meet the target profile, it validated several critical systems:

  1. Mechanical Robustness: The 3D-printed landing legs and gimbal mount remained structurally intact despite high ignition heat.
  2. Software Execution: The PID control loop successfully initialized and began processing sensor data prior to the thermal failure.

6. The Road Ahead: Future Iterations

The lessons learned from GARUD have opened the door for several advanced enhancements:

  • Reaction Wheels: Integrating internal rotating masses to provide active roll control.
  • Canard Control: Adding small, forward-mounted fins to enhance maneuverability during the descent phase.
  • Motor Optimization: Transitioning to motors with higher average thrust and lower peak thrust to improve overall stability during the initial liftoff.

Summary of Technical Competencies:

  • Aerospace: Dynamics Modeling, Stability Analysis, Thrust Vectoring.
  • Control Systems: PID Algorithm Development, Sensor Fusion (IMU/Altimeter), MATLAB/Simulink.
  • Mechanical: CAD Design (Fusion 360), Additive Manufacturing (Cura), Structural Testing.
  • Electronics: Embedded Systems (C++), PCB Design, Data Acquisition.
