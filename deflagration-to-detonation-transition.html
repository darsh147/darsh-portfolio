Mastering the "Explosion within an Explosion": The CFD of Deflagration-to-Detonation Transition (DDT)

Propulsion Research

Simulation of Supersonic Combustion

In the high-stakes world of aerospace engineering, the difference between a controlled burn and a catastrophic failure—or a revolutionary new engine—often comes down to the speed of a flame. This blog post dives into my research at IIT Bombay on the Deflagration-to-Detonation Transition (DDT), a phenomenon where a subsonic flame accelerates into a supersonic detonation wave.

1. The Core Challenge: Subsonic vs. Supersonic

At its simplest, combustion happens in two regimes:

  • Deflagration: A subsonic process where heat transfer and diffusion drive the flame (think of a gas stove).
  • Detonation: A supersonic powerhouse (1,500–2,500 m/s) where a leading shock wave compresses the gas so violently it auto-ignites.

Understanding how one turns into the other is critical for Pulse Detonation Engines (PDEs), which offer a pressure-gain combustion cycle that can enhance thrust performance by up to 30% compared to traditional cycles.

2. The Thermodynamic Selection Rules

To model DDT, we use the Rankine-Hugoniot relations to map out every possible final state of the gas.

The Hugoniot & Rayleigh Balance

A stable detonation exists only at the intersection of two critical mathematical constraints:

The Hugoniot Curve

The locus of all thermodynamically possible post-wave states based on the First Law (Energy Conservation).

(P2/P1 + (γ-1)/(γ+1)) · (v1/v2 - (γ-1)/(γ+1)) = 4γ/(γ+1)2 + (2γq/a12) · (γ-1)/(γ+1)

The Rayleigh Line

A mechanical constraint derived from Mass and Momentum conservation.

P2/P1 - 1 = γM12 · (1 - v1/v2)
Recruiter Insight: My analysis focused on the Chapman-Jouguet (C-J) condition, the point where the Rayleigh line is tangent to the Hugoniot curve. This represents the only stable, self-sustaining detonation state where the flow behind the wave is sonic (M2 = 1)—preventing pressure disturbances from "catching up" to and weakening the shock front.

3. The ZND Model: Visualizing the Internal Structure

Real-world detonations aren't infinitely thin lines. My simulation utilized the Zeldovich-von Neumann-Döring (ZND) model to resolve the wave's internal architecture:

  • 1
    The von Neumann Spike: A non-reactive shock that compresses the unburnt gas to extreme pressures.
  • 2
    The Reaction Zone: A finite region where species like H2 are consumed to form H2O.

4. Advanced CFD Methodology for 2026

Modeling DDT requires capturing a "self-reinforcing feedback loop" where turbulence wrinkles the flame, increasing its surface area and heat release, which in turn creates stronger shocks.

My Simulation Setup (ANSYS Fluent)

  • Geometry: A 1.0-meter obstructed tube with 8 mm obstacles designed to mimic the blockage ratio of a Pulse Detonation Rocket Engine (PDRE).
  • Turbulence (DDES): I employed Delayed Detached Eddy Simulation, a hybrid RANS-LES model. In 2025-2026, DDES has become the gold standard for high-speed reactive flows because it provides LES-level accuracy in turbulent flame regions while maintaining RANS efficiency at the walls.
  • Combustion: Used the Species Transport model with a Finite-Rate solver to track detailed chemical kinetics in a Hydrogen-Air mixture.

5. Results: Validating the Supersonic Shock

The simulation successfully captured the transition, with results closely matching theoretical ZND/CJ predictions:

Parameter Theoretical (CJ/ZND) My Simulated Value Accuracy
Peak Pressure (PVN) 25 atm 22.5 atm -10% error
Mach Number 4.8 4.4 -8% error
CJ Pressure (PCJ) 15 atm 17 atm +13% error

The mass fraction contours proved the success of the model: at the shock front, H2 was at its peak, while H2O was zero, followed by a rapid conversion in the reaction zone. This confirms a true detonation rather than a decoupled shock and deflagration.

6. Why This Matters: The Global Energy Transition

As of 2026, the industry is pivoting toward Carbon-Neutral Energy Production. Projects like ReDDT are currently using these exact numerical methods to study hydrogen safety in industrial settings. Whether it’s designing next-gen hypersonic propulsion or ensuring safety in hydrogen storage facilities, the ability to predict and control DDT is a top-tier engineering skill.

The Bottom Line: By leveraging high-resolution CFD and deep thermodynamic theory, I have demonstrated that we can accurately predict the onset of supersonic detonation—a key unlock for future aerospace and energy systems.
